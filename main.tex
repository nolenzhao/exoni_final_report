% --- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (Front Matter, 4%) ---
\section*{Executive Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Executive Summary}

Exoni is a powered knee exoskeleton designed for outdoor recreational use by mobility-limited individuals. The core engineering challenge is that a wearable knee actuator must simultaneously deliver high output torque, remain backdrivable when unpowered, stay cool enough not to cause discomfort, and fit within a strict mass budget to keep the goal of aiding the user. No off-the-shelf motor or control system satisfied all four requirements, which drove 
the creation of fully custom mechanical, electrical, and firmware design.

The mechanical subsystem is built around a custom brushless DC motor in an 8110 stator frame with a Halbach array rotor, coupled to an 8.4:1 planetary gearbox. The electrical subsystem comprises three custom PCBAs (primary controller, encoder, and IMU boards) and an 8S1P lithium-ion battery pack. The firmware runs on an STM32G474 microcontroller under FreeRTOS, implementing a 1~kHz Field Oriented Control (FOC) inner loop and a 200Hz gait-adaptive impedance control outer loop. Gait phase (pre-swing, stance, and swing) is classified in real time on-device by a random forest model compiled directly into firmware, allowing the impedance controller to adapt its spring-damper parameters to the current phase of the user's stride.

All mechanical and firmware design constraints were met. 
The gearbox delivers 17.6Nm output torque, backdrive resistance is 0.26Nm, and peak motor surface temperature under sustained load is 35~\textdegree{}C against a 43~\textdegree{}C skin-contact safety limit. 
The complete system weighs 1.5~kg as targeted, with the motor alone at 582~g against a 600~g budget. 
All seven firmware constraints were verified; closed-loop FOC was confirmed with $i_d \to 0$ and $i_q$ tracking its reference, 
and the gait classifier runs stably at 200Hz with phase output confirmed in bench test logs and telemetry.
The simulation-driven design process was essential to achieving these results. Electromagnetic and thermal simulations guided the key design decision. 
Stator frame selection, rotor magnetic configuration, and battery cell chemistry were resolved correctly through simulation. 
The platform is validated at the subsystem level and is suitable for use by those suffering from injuries.

% --- 1. INTRODUCTION (20%) ---
\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background}
Exoni is an open-source powered knee exoskeleton designed for outdoor recreational use. The system targets mobility-limited users who wish to maintain their hiking and running intensity — functioning analogously to an e-bike, but for lower-limb assistance. Unlike clinical rehabilitation exoskeletons, which are designed for indoor, low-speed, supervised environments, an outdoor exoskeleton must operate across variable terrain, tolerate ambient temperature variation, and remain safe and comfortable for extended wear. This imposes a distinct set of engineering constraints: the device must maximize power density within a small form factor, dissipate heat passively while remaining cool to the touch, and remain transparent to the user's natural gait when unpowered (backdrivable).

The core actuation challenge stems from the human knee joint, which requires peak torques on the order of 0.5 - 1 Nm/kg during stair climbing and loaded hiking. For a representative user mass, this drives a gearbox output torque requirement of approximately 20 Nm (or around 40\% of knee torque). Achieving this with a motor small enough to mount at the knee joint, light enough to avoid gait disruption, and cool enough to maintain skin-contact safety requires a custom motor design — off-the-shelf motors at this power level are either too large, too heavy, or thermally unviable for wearable use.

In order to power the exoskeleton, including the motor, an electrical system needs to exist. Off-the-shelf boards do not exist that meet requirements such as reading from multiple IMUs, running FOC control on the motor, sending telemetry data wirelessly, and more. Therefore, the electrical system requires the use of custom PCBs (printed circuit board) that are designed to meet the requirements in order to have a working knee exoskeleton. Equally, the firmware running on those boards must meet real-time constraints that no off-the-shelf embedded framework satisfies out of the box: the motor drive loop must execute at 1~kHz to maintain stable FOC commutation, the current-sense ADCs must be calibrated and synchronized with the PWM carrier, and the encoder must be read and decoded within the same tick. This necessitated a custom firmware architecture built on FreeRTOS, with a dedicated high-priority task for motor control and a separate task for the outer impedance control loop.

\subsection{Needs Assessment \& Problem Formulation}
The target user population is individuals with mild-to-moderate lower limb weakness or fatigue, including older adults experiencing age-related strength decline, 
injured people in looking to regain independence, and able-bodied users under sustained high-load conditions such as multi-day backpacking or trail running. 
Lower limb muscle weakness is highly prevalent: quadriceps strength declines by approximately 1--2\% per year after age 50, and knee osteoarthritis affects roughly 10\% of adults over 55, 
with significantly higher rates among recreational hikers and workers who spend extended time on uneven terrain. For these users, 
a powered knee assist device that reduces the muscular effort required during the stance and swing phases of the gait cycle could meaningfully extend activity range and reduce injury risk.

The closest commercially available product targeting this use case is the Skip MO/GO, a powered knee exoskeleton designed for outdoor hiking and elevation assistance. However, at approximately \$5{,}000, the MO/GO is financially out of reach for the average recreational user, leaving the broader population who could benefit from powered gait assistance without an accessible option.

The core unmet need is therefore a self-contained, lightweight, and affordable, powered knee assist device that provides meaningful torque during loaded gait, 
remains transparent to natural movement when unpowered, and is safe for extended skin contact without active cooling. 
The problem to solve is to design and build a powered knee exoskeleton with an integrated custom motor and drivetrain that meets the constraints specified in Section~\ref{sec:constraints}, 
with particular emphasis on the conflicting demands of high torque output, low backdrive resistance, passive thermal safety, minimal mass, and responsive controls.

The 1 Nm requirement for backdrive torque is a limit for a system to feel backdrivable to a human. 43C is the maximum temperature that still feels comfortable. These constraints directly conflict. Higher output torque requires higher current draw, which increases both heat generation (scaling as $I^2 R$) and cogging torque (increasing backdrive resistance). Reducing mass reduces the thermal mass available for heat storage and the cross-sectional area available for conduction paths. These tradeoffs cannot be resolved by intuition alone: simulation was required to inform both the electromagnetic and thermal design of the motor and battery pack.

\vspace{10px}
The firmware and control constraints are driven by the real-time demands of field-oriented control and impedance-based gait assistance. The 1~kHz FOC inner loop rate is the minimum required to maintain stable current regulation with the motor's electrical time constant; slower loop rates introduce phase error that degrades torque linearity and risks instability at high speeds. 
The 15~A phase overcurrent trip threshold is set conservatively below the motor's thermal limit to protect against fault conditions while allowing full-load operation. 
The 200Hz control cycle constraint ensures that logged data is coherent with the control state and remains responsive enough for each phase of the gait cycle. It sits in between
being responsive enough for the actual control algorithm as well as slow enough for telemetry logging.

The design pipeline proceeded through five sequential stages, each feeding quantitative results forward to the next:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Electromagnetic analysis:} Ansys Maxwell simulations were used to select the stator frame (7210 vs.\ 8110) and rotor magnetic configuration (iron-backed, ironless, and multiple Halbach array geometries) based on torque constant ($K_t$, in Nm/A) and motor constant ($K_m$, in Nm/$\sqrt{\text{W}}$), the latter being the relevant figure of merit for a thermally constrained motor.

    \item \textbf{Motor thermal analysis:} SimScale conjugate heat transfer (CHT) simulations assessed whether passive cooling could maintain surface temperatures below 43~\textdegree{}C under the electromagnetic loss inputs from Stage 1. Iterative geometry modifications were evaluated.

    \item \textbf{Battery thermal analysis:} SimScale CHT simulations evaluated the effect of battery pack configuration (series vs.\ parallel cell arrangement) and housing geometry on cell surface temperatures, with a 35~\textdegree{}C safety limit.

    \item \textbf{Firmware and control design:} A custom FreeRTOS firmware was developed for the STM32G474, implementing a 1~kHz Field Oriented Control (FOC) inner loop and a 200Hz impedance control outer loop. Key decisions included the dual-task scheduling architecture, the SVPWM modulation strategy with third-harmonic injection, and the encoder electrical angle alignment procedure. PI current controller gains were tuned analytically and verified against the live telemetry stream.

    \item \textbf{Final design:} Outputs from all four stages were consolidated into the final hardware and software specification.

\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Halbach Array BLDC Motor Design}

\paragraph{Stator Selection}

The electromagnetic design began with a selection between two commercially available stator frames: the 7210 frame (72~mm diameter, 36 slots, 7 turns per tooth) and the 8110 frame (81~mm diameter, 24 slots, 13 turns per tooth). Ansys Maxwell's analytical equation solver was used to compute the motor constants for each configuration, with wire turns per slot limited by the physical copper fill factor of each slot geometry.

The relevant figure of merit for a thermally constrained wearable motor is the motor constant $K_m = K_t / \sqrt{R}$ (in Nm/$\sqrt{\text{W}}$), which quantifies torque output per unit of heat generated. Although the 7210 frame yields an 11\% higher torque constant ($K_t = 0.124$~Nm/A vs.\ $0.112$~Nm/A for the 8110), its higher phase resistance means it produces 23\% more heat for the same torque --- a motor constant of 0.399 vs.\ 0.444 for the 8110. Accordingly, the \textbf{8110 frame} was selected as the basis for the motor design.

Model verification used the analytical relationship $K_t \propto N_\text{phase} \times d_\text{stator}$, where $N_\text{phase}$ is the total turns per phase. The predicted relative torque constants ($7210: 13 \times 8 \times 72 = 7488$; $8110: 7 \times 12 \times 81 = 6804$) were consistent with the simulated values, confirming the model.

\paragraph{Rotor Configuration}

Having fixed the stator, the rotor magnetic configuration was selected to optimise the tradeoff between torque constant ($K_t$) and backdrivability (quantified by peak cogging torque). Five configurations were evaluated using 2D Ansys Maxwell transient motor simulations: iron-backed, ironless, and three Halbach array geometries with varying main and auxiliary magnet dimensions. Input data comprised measured magnet dimensions and datasheet performance characteristics (remanence, coercivity).

The key assumption underpinning the backdrivability analysis is that gearbox friction is minimised through the 8.4:1 planetary gearbox design, making backdrive resistance predominantly a function of magnetic cogging torque rather than mechanical friction.

Halbach arrays concentrate magnetic flux on the airgap-facing side of the rotor by varying magnet orientation, eliminating the need for a heavy steel back-iron yoke. This simultaneously reduces rotor mass and, by reducing the flux linkage variation with rotor angle that drives cogging, lowers peak cogging torque. \subsubsection{Firmware and Software}
\label{sec:firmware}

The STM32G474 firmware is written in C and runs under FreeRTOS 10.3.1 with a 1~kHz scheduler tick. Two tasks divide the real-time workload: a high-priority sensor/motor task running at 1~kHz and a normal-priority control task running at 200Hz.

\paragraph{FreeRTOS Task Architecture}

\textbf{StartDefaultTask} executes at 1~kHz and is the highest-priority task on the system. Each iteration reads the AS5048A magnetic encoder via SPI, converts the 14-bit raw count to a mechanical angle (rad), and computes the electrical angle by scaling by the motor's pole-pair count. Every fifth iteration (200Hz), it reads both LSM6DSVETR IMUs and writes their accelerometer and gyroscope snapshots to volatile shared buffers, then issues an \texttt{xTaskNotifyGive} to wake the ControlTask. On each 1~kHz tick it calls \texttt{Motor\_Step}, which advances the FOC commutation loop and writes new PWM duty cycles to TIM1. The task also monitors the DRV8323 fault pin (\texttt{MTR\_nFAULT}): if a fault is detected it disables the motor enable line and retries every 500~ms. Two telemetry streams are produced: a human-readable CSV log at 100~Hz over UART2 to the ESP32, and a compact 43-byte binary frame at 200Hz containing encoder angle, both IMU readings, the current torque command, and a CRC-8 integrity check.

\textbf{ControlTask} runs at 200Hz and is woken exclusively by \texttt{xTaskNotifyTake}, ensuring it executes only when fresh IMU data is available. It reads the latest IMU and encoder snapshots, computes knee angular velocity via difference, applies an impedance control law to produce a torque command, converts that torque to a q-axis current reference, and writes the result to a shared volatile variable consumed by the FOC loop.

\paragraph{Field Oriented Control}

FOC decouples the motor's torque-producing and flux-producing current components, enabling precise torque control by regulating two DC quantities (the d- and q-axis currents) rather than three sinusoidal phase currents. The control loop executes at 1~kHz inside \texttt{Motor\_Step}.

Phase currents are measured on two of the three motor phases using 1~m$\Omega$ shunt resistors and the DRV8323's integrated current-sense amplifiers at a gain of 20~V/V. The DRV8323 is a gate driver and current-sense amplifier IC from Texas Instruments, selected for its integrated three-phase gate driver, adjustable current-sense gain, and hardware over-current protection. Outputs are digitised by ADC3 and ADC4 on the STM32, each running at 12-bit resolution with 47.5-cycle sampling. Current offset calibration (\texttt{FOC\_CalibrateOffsets}) averages 64 ADC readings at zero current to remove amplifier bias from all subsequent measurements.

The Clarke transform converts the two measured phase currents $i_a$ and $i_b$ to the stationary $\alpha$-$\beta$ frame, exploiting Kirchhoff's current law ($i_c = -(i_a + i_b)$):
\begin{align}
    i_\alpha &= i_a \\
    i_\beta  &= \frac{i_a + 2i_b}{\sqrt{3}}
\end{align}

The Park transform then rotates the stationary frame into the rotor-synchronous $d$-$q$ frame using the electrical angle $\theta_e$:
\begin{align}
    i_d &=  i_\alpha \cos\theta_e + i_\beta \sin\theta_e \\
    i_q &= -i_\alpha \sin\theta_e + i_\beta \cos\theta_e
\end{align}

Two independent PI controllers close the loop on $i_d$ and $i_q$ separately, each running at 1~kHz with a timestep of $\Delta t = 1$~ms and anti-windup integrator clamping. For torque control the d-axis reference is held at zero (no flux weakening) and the q-axis reference is set by the outer impedance loop. The PI gains used for the custom 15-pole-pair motor are $K_{p,d} = 0.3$, $K_{i,d} = 1.0$, $K_{p,q} = 1.0$, $K_{i,q} = 100.0$, with output limits of $\pm$24~V corresponding to the half-bus voltage. An over-current trip at 15~A per phase immediately zeros all PWM outputs and resets the PI integrators.

The PI voltage outputs are transformed back to the stationary frame via the inverse Park transform, then converted to three-phase duty cycles using Space Vector PWM (SVPWM) with third-harmonic common-mode injection. Common-mode injection extends the linear modulation range to approximately 15\% beyond sine-triangle PWM for the same DC bus voltage:
\begin{equation}
    v_\text{cm} = -\frac{\max(v_a, v_b, v_c) + \min(v_a, v_b, v_c)}{2}
\end{equation}
The resulting duty cycles are written to TIM1's capture/compare registers. TIM1 is configured in centre-aligned mode at 20~kHz (ARR = 399 at 16~MHz) with 500~ns of dead time on each complementary output pair to prevent shoot-through in the three half-bridge legs of the DRV8323.

\paragraph{Encoder and Angle Calibration}

The AS5048A is a 14-bit magnetic rotary encoder read over SPI at Mode 1 (CPOL = 0, CPHA = 1) using a pipelined read protocol: each transaction issues the angle register address (0x3FFF) and simultaneously receives the result of the previous request, reducing latency by one SPI frame. The 14-bit raw count spans 0--16383 counts per revolution (0.022$^\circ$/count, $3.84 \times 10^{-4}$~rad/count).

Before closed-loop operation, an electrical angle alignment routine maps the encoder frame to the motor's electrical frame. The STM32 applies a fixed d-axis voltage at electrical angle $\theta_e = -\pi/2$ for one second, locking the rotor to Phase~A's magnetic zero. The encoder angle at this position, scaled by the pole-pair count, gives the electrical offset $\theta_\text{offset}$ that is subtracted from all subsequent angle computations:
\begin{equation}
    \theta_e = \theta_\text{mech} \times P_p - \theta_\text{offset}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Motor Control Modes and Soft-Start}

The firmware supports four motor control modes managed by \texttt{motor\_cntrl.c}: \texttt{MOTOR\_MODE\_OFF} (PWM zeroed), \texttt{MOTOR\_MODE\_OPEN\_LOOP} (fixed $V_q$ with internal angle ramp, independent of encoder), \texttt{MOTOR\_MODE\_CLOSED\_LOOP} (FOC with PI current control at a fixed $I_q$ reference), and \texttt{MOTOR\_MODE\_IMPEDANCE} (outer impedance loop sets $I_q$). At startup, the system enters open-loop mode and ramps the q-axis voltage linearly from 0 to 3.0~V over 200ms to limit inrush current and allow the motor to establish rotation before encoder-based commutation takes over.

\paragraph{Impedance Control Outer Loop}

The 200Hz ControlTask implements a two-step virtual impedance law. First, a spring-damper equation computes a target joint torque from the measured knee state:
\begin{equation}
    \tau = K(\theta_\text{des} - \theta) + B(\dot{\theta}_\text{des} - \dot{\theta})
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the virtual stiffness (Nm/deg), $B$ is the virtual damping (Nm$\cdot$s/deg), $\theta$ is the measured knee angle, $\theta_\text{des}$ is the phase reference angle, and $\dot{\theta}_\text{des}$, $\dot{\theta}$ are the reference and measured knee angular velocities. A backdrive friction compensation term $\tau_\text{bd} = \pm0.26$~Nm is then added to overcome passive gearbox resistance (applied when $|\dot{\theta}| > 5$~deg/s; zero within the deadband to avoid sign-flip). The compensated torque is converted to a q-axis current reference:
\begin{equation}
    I_q = \frac{\tau + \tau_\text{bd}}{K_t \cdot N_\text{gear}}
\end{equation}
where $K_t = 0.97$~Nm/A and $N_\text{gear} = 8.4$. The output is clamped to $\pm10$~A before being passed to the FOC inner loop, which regulates the physical phase currents to track it.

\paragraph{Telemetry}

The STM32 streams two data formats over UART2 at 921600~baud to the ESP32-S3. A 43-byte binary frame transmitted at 200Hz contains synchronisation bytes, a sequence counter, encoder angle in centidegrees, six-axis IMU data from both sensors (accelerometer in units of $10^{-4}$~m/s$^2$, gyroscope in units of $10^{-3}$~deg/s), knee angular velocity, torque command, q-axis current command, and a CRC-8 integrity byte. A human-readable CSV stream at 100~Hz logs electrical angle, phase currents, PI outputs, raw ADC counts, calibration offsets, and the fault pin state for offline debugging. The ESP32 forwards this data wirelessly and supports OTA firmware updates over Wi-Fi, decoupling the real-time STM32 loop from all wireless communication latency.

\paragraph{Machine Learning Gait Classification}

The machine learning gait classification is currently built to handle a single type of activity, incline walking. 
The random forest is built to split the incline walking activity into 3 distinct phases, pre-swing, swing, and stance. 
The canonical example of incline walking is ascending a flight of stairs. The following described the qualitative characteristics 
of each phase.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Pre-swing: Leg is bent and applying force onto the step to raise the body up
    \item Swing: Leg freely in the air and is rapidly being raised to be placed onto the next step
    \item Stance: Leg is bearing most of the bodies load, and is fully straight 
\end{itemize}

Once the random forest classifier identifies the appropriate phase based on the input sensor and motor encoder data,
the spring damper constants used for the impedance controller are grabbed from a lookup table based on the phase. As described
above, the spring damper constants used will be different for each phase, as well as each desired knee angle $\theta_\text{des}$ and desired angular velocity $\dot{\theta}_\text{des}$. The per-phase values are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:phase_params}.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Per-Phase Impedance Parameters}
\label{tab:phase_params}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Phase} & \textbf{$K$ (Nm/deg)} & \textbf{$B$ (Nm$\cdot$s/deg)} & \textbf{$\theta_\text{des}$ (deg)} & \textbf{$\dot{\theta}_\text{des}$ (deg/s)} \\
\midrule
PRE\_SWING & 0.08 & 0.001 & 20.0 & 30.0 \\
STANCE     & 0.15 & 0.002 &  0.0 &  0.0 \\
SWING      & 0.02 & 0.001 & 75.0 & 80.0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The values were tuned based on the desired qualitative behaviour in each phase of the gait cycle. In the table, 
the pre-swing phase has a desired thigh-calf target angle of $20 \degree$ to move the leg towards a standing position, 
and a moderate velocity was used to help straighten the leg without violently jerking. The stance phase aims to help 
maintain a straight leg, with no movement so both reference target angle and velocity are zeroed. Finally, swing phase has 
the fastest velocity as the leg rapidly moves out of the stance phase and onto the next step, bending sharply to $75 \degree$ 
in the process. This was done because a fixed spring-damper constant cannot generalize to all highly unique characteristics of
different phases. The classifier output selects which row of this table is active at each 200Hz tick, and those parameters are passed directly into the impedance law and torque-to-current conversion described above.


\vspace{10px}
Software was written to poll the IMU sensors and motor encoder in a 200Hz loop to derive samples every 5ms from the STM32 and transmitted over 
UART to the ESP32, which streamed over WiFi to an external laptop which contained a server that listened for these packets. This was done rather 
than record samples on device to ensure the limited flash memory on the STM32 or ESP32 would not be filled, as numerous trials were run. 
Simultaneously, a motion capture (MOCAP) system records the same trials to provide ground-truth labels for training. The MOCAP output is a biomechanical model result sampled at 200Hz, producing 174 columns per frame. These include bilateral joint angles (flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, internal/external rotation) at the ankle, knee, hip, and pelvis; bilateral joint reaction forces and moments at each joint; bilateral joint powers; and discrete gait event channels marking foot contact and foot-off timing for each leg. 
This data is more extensive than Exoni's on-device sensor stream and is used exclusively for labelling. 
The MOCAP-derived gait phase labels are aligned to the simultaneously recorded IMU and encoder samples, providing the supervised training signal. 
The MOCAP data must be cleaned prior to labelling to drop corrupted frames and position outliers. 
A heuristic is developed to label the MOCAP data. 
It is based off of the maximum point of extension of the leg as captured by the knee angle column (\texttt{LKneeAngles}), 
combined with the foot contact event markers (\texttt{LFO}, \texttt{LTO}) that record the precise timing of foot-off and toe-off for each stride. 
A local minimum in knee flexion angle, which corresponds to maximum extension marks the \verb|STANCE phase|. 
The interval from that extension peak to the subsequent foot-off event is labelled PRE\_SWING, as the leg is still bearing load but beginning to unload. 
From foot-off until the knee reaches peak flexion on the following step is labelled SWING. 
This is applied across all trials and then aligned to the concurrently recorded IMU and encoder samples, which 
is the cross-referencing mentioned earlier. The MOCAP data acts as the ground truth.
producing a labelled dataset where each 5~ms sample carries one of the three phase labels used to train the random forest.

\vspace{10px}

Before training, the raw sensor stream is expanded into a 45-element feature vector. The first 13 features are instantaneous readings: the encoder angle and the six-axis IMU output from both sensors. Six derived scalar features are then computed from the current sample: encoder angular velocity (difference), the $\ell_2$ acceleration magnitude of each IMU, and the component-wise gyroscope difference between the two IMUs (capturing relative angular velocity between thigh and lower leg). The remaining 26 features are \textbf{windowed statistics} — the per-channel mean and standard deviation computed over a 10-sample (10*5 = 50ms) sliding window applied to the encoder angle and all six axes of both IMUs.

The windowed statistics are the most important addition to the feature set. 
Instantaneous sensor values at phase boundaries are often ambiguous and could go either way. 
The knee angle and IMU readings at the moment the foot leaves the ground look similar to those a few milliseconds before or after. 
The 50ms window mean and standard deviation capture the local trend and time series temporal data of each signal, giving the classifier enough temporal context to distinguish transitions. 
On the STM32, the window is implemented as a circular buffer (CB) of 10 raw samples. The mean and standard deviation are computed at each tick with no heap allocation due to static CB size. 
The complete feature vector is featured in Appendix~\ref{sec:gait_feature_vector}.

The labeled dataset is then used to train a \verb|RandomForestClassifier|.
Several techniques were applied to improve accuracy and prevent overfitting.
First, a group-aware train/test split ensures all samples from a single trial stay entirely within either the training or test partition.
This is critical because consecutive 5~ms frames within one trial are temporally correlated. If using a naive random split, the model could memorize trial-specific patterns from timestamps and report artificially high accuracy.
Second, K-fold cross-validation is used, keeping trial groups intact across folds for the same reason.
Third, the classifier is trained to compensate for unequal phase durations inherent in stair gait.
Given that stance occupies a larger fraction of each cycle than pre-swing or swing, the model is reweighted to avoid bias towards the majority class.
The features are also normalized to have similar dynamic range among them for preparation for quantization, where values are scaled to \texttt{int16} by multiplying by a per-feature scalar derived from the training set's maximum absolute value (see Appendix~\ref{sec:gait_feature_vector}).
The trained forest is then exported to a C header via \verb|emlearn|, producing a fully inlined header file compiled directly into firmware flash.
This allows classifier weights to be loaded onto the stack, bypassing heap allocation and its associated unpredictable timing.

Following these steps to develop the model, the prediction accuracy is about 87\% for all three gait phases.

\subsection{Modifications and Deviations from Original Design}

Four significant deviations were made from the MTE~481 proposed firmware and control design.

\paragraph{Phase-Adaptive Impedance Control.}

The original design proposed a single fixed set of impedance parameters ($K$, $B$, $\theta_\text{ref}$) applied uniformly throughout the gait cycle.
In practice, a single parameter set cannot simultaneously provide extension assistance during stance and low resistance during swing, 
the same stiffness that helps push the knee to full extension during weight-bearing would actively resist the motion of the leg in the air.
The final implementation dispatches three independent parameter sets (PRE\_SWING, STANCE, SWING) selected in real time by the gait classifier, allowing the controller to provide assistive torque only when and how it is needed.

\paragraph{Random Forest Classifier Over Rule-Based Gait Detection.}

The original design specified a threshold-based phase detector using encoder angle crossings and a velocity sign check.
This approach is sensitive to sensor noise and does not generalise across subjects or terrain variations such as stair height.
The final implementation replaces the rule-based detector with a random forest classifier trained on MOCAP-labelled stair-ascent data, which demonstrated improved robustness across trial conditions.
The switch was enabled by the availability of motion-capture ground-truth labels and the \verb|emlearn| framework for generating inline C.

\paragraph{Sliding-Window Feature Engineering Over Instantaneous Features.}

The original design was feeding instantaneous sensor readings directly into the gait classifier at each 200Hz tick.
In practice, phase boundaries are ambiguous in for single samples. 
The encoder angle and IMU values at the moment the foot leaves the ground are not reliably distinguishable from those a few milliseconds before or after.
The final implementation augments the 13 element instantaneous single sample feature vector with 26 additional windowed statistics. 
These are per-channel mean and standard deviation over a 10-sample sliding window, which brings the total feature count to 45.
The window is maintained as a circular buffer.
This change alone was responsible for the largest single improvement in classifier accuracy during development even more than adding training samples.

\paragraph{Two-Layer Current Safety Architecture.}
The original design specified a single current limit enforced in firmware.
The final implementation distinguishes two independent protection layers: a hardware overcurrent protection trip at 15~A configured in the DRV8323 gate driver (which requires a fault-clear sequence), and a software torque guard of $\pm$18~Nm with an additional current clamp of $\pm$10~A enforced within the impedance controller.
The hardware layer now protects against firmware faults while the software layer prevents extreme torque 
due to sensor noise or classifier error without triggering a full gate-drive shutdown.

\subsubsection{Firmware Flashing \& Telemetry Check}
\label{sec:fw_commissioning}

Firmware was compiled using the STM32CubeIDE toolchain (GCC ARM) and flashed to the STM32G474RE via SWD (Serial Wire Debug) using an ST-Link V3 programmer integrated on the primary board. After each flash, the UART2 CSV telemetry stream was monitored at 921600~baud using a serial terminal to confirm that sensor reads were live and values were sensible before proceeding to motor tests.

The first commissioning step was ADC current offset calibration. With the motor unpowered and all PWM outputs disabled, \texttt{FOC\_CalibrateOffsets} was called to average 64 ADC samples on both current-sense channels and store the zero-current offsets. The resulting offsets were printed over UART and inspected to confirm they fell within the expected mid-rail range of the DRV8323 current-sense amplifiers.

The encoder was verified by manually rotating the motor shaft and confirming that the reported mechanical angle in the telemetry stream increased and decreased monotonically over a full revolution, and that the 14-bit count wrapped cleanly at 0/16383. The AS5048A diagnostic register was also read to confirm the OCF (offset compensation finished) bit was set, indicating the internal offset compensation algorithm had converged.

The electrical angle alignment routine was then executed to calibrate the offset between the encoder frame and the motor's electrical frame. With the motor stationary and suspended (no load), the STM32 applied a fixed d-axis voltage for one second to lock the rotor to Phase~A's magnetic zero. The resulting \texttt{theta\_offset} value was stored and validated by stepping the motor through one open-loop electrical revolution and confirming that the commutation produced smooth rotation without vibration or stalling.

Open-loop spin-up was tested next. The motor was commanded into open-loop mode with the q-axis voltage ramp active, and the telemetry stream was used to observe the soft-start ramp reaching 3.0~V over 200ms with no phase overcurrent trips. The motor accelerated smoothly to the open-loop target speed, confirming that the SVPWM output, dead-time insertion, and DRV8323 gate drive were all functioning correctly.

Closed-loop FOC commissioning was performed by switching to \texttt{MOTOR\_MODE\_CLOSED\_LOOP} at a low $I_q$ reference (0.5~A) and monitoring the d- and q-axis currents in the telemetry CSV. Correct closed-loop operation was confirmed when $i_d$ converged to near zero and $i_q$ tracked the reference with low steady-state error, indicating proper encoder-to-electrical-frame alignment and well-tuned PI gains. The DRV8323 fault pin state (\texttt{nfault} column in the CSV) remained high throughout, confirming no gate driver faults were triggered.


\subsection{Testing and Performance}

\paragraph{Firmware and Control Performance}

All seven firmware and control constraints from Table~\ref{tab:fw_constraints} were verified during commissioning. The FOC inner loop executes at exactly 1~kHz using \texttt{vTaskDelayUntil} against TIM1 with ARR~$= 399$ at a 400~MHz clock, giving a 20~kHz center-aligned PWM carrier with no jitter attributable to task preemption. The 200Hz impedance control outer loop is synchronised to the IMU data-ready interrupt via \texttt{xTaskNotifyGive}, so it never executes on stale sensor data. The AS5048A magnetic encoder provides 14-bit resolution (16\,384 counts per revolution, $0.022^\circ$/count), and phase currents are digitised by the STM32G474's 12-bit ADC3/ADC4 in simultaneous-sampling mode. Hardware overcurrent protection is configured in the DRV8323 gate driver at 15~A; no nFAULT events were triggered during any test session. In closed-loop operation at a commanded $I_{q,\text{ref}} = 0.5$~A, $i_d$ converged to near zero and $i_q$ tracked the reference with low steady-state error, confirming correct Park/Clarke transform orientation and PI controller tuning.
\vspace{10px}

A critical-path analysis confirmed that on-chip sensor reads and random forest inference both impose negligible computational cost: the classifier is compiled to a fully inlined C header via \texttt{emlearn} and executes from flash with no heap allocation, well within the 5ms loop budget. 
The binding constraint unexpectedly arises from communication bandwidth. 
An alternative architecture that offloads gait classification to the ESP32 co-processor was evaluated and rejected due to the round-trip time (RTT) cost. 
At 921\,600~baud, serialising a full sensor frame containing three-axis accelerometer, three-axis gyroscope, and motor encoder over UART and waiting for a response limits the effective inference rate. 
Transferring one sensor frame from the STM32 to the ESP32 using the UART protocol is calculated below in Equation \ref{eq:baud_rate}.
\begin{equation}
    t_{\text{frame}} = \frac{N_{\text{fields}} \times 4~\text{bytes} \times 10~\text{bits/byte}}{f_{\text{baud}}} = \frac{7 \times 4 \times 10}{921{,}600} \approx 0.304~\text{ms}
    \label{eq:baud_rate}
\end{equation}
This is one-way, so the round-trip cost before any inference begins is at minimum $\sim$0.6~ms. In addition, the task overhead of the ESP32 servicing Wi-Fi communication to external listeners can cause temporary spikes in latency, making the effective inference rate inconsistent. The random forest was trained on features extracted at a fixed 200Hz rate; running inference at a faster, non-uniform rate would introduce a resolution mismatch between training and deployment, artificially degrading classifier accuracy and causing the impedance controller to perform unpredictably. All gait classification and impedance computation therefore executes on the STM32G474, 
keeping the full pipeline at a steady 200Hz with the ESP32 used only as a passive wireless telemetry bridge for analysis and debugging.

\paragraph{Motor Thermal Performance}

The motor thermal simulation used SimScale's conjugate heat transfer (CHT) solver, which simultaneously models conduction through the stator and housing, natural and forced internal convection, external forced convection (modelled at walking speed), and radiation from the outer housing surface. Heat inputs were drawn from the prior electromagnetic simulation: 5~W of hysteresis and eddy current losses, and 17~W of resistive ($I^2R$) winding losses at rated current draw. A bulk winding thermal conductivity of 0.5~W/m$\cdot$K was used, significantly below that of pure copper, to account for air gaps between wires and epoxy-fill variations.

The baseline simulation predicted a surface temperature of 81~\textdegree{}C, well above the 43~\textdegree{}C target. Three sequential geometry modifications were applied and re-simulated:

\paragraph{Battery Thermal Performance}

The battery pack is mounted at the user's hip and must remain below 35~\textdegree{}C. The primary design variable was the cell configuration: more cells in parallel reduces per-cell current and thus $I^2R$ losses, but increases weight. Numerical analysis of relative heat losses across configurations (4s2p, 5s2p, 6s2p, 7s2p, 8s1p) using Molicel P45B 21700 cell internal resistance and nickel strip interconnect resistance showed that the 8s1p configuration has the highest relative heat loss per cell but the lowest total cell count and weight. The 8s1p case was therefore simulated as the worst case.

The SimScale CHT model included anisotropic cell thermal conductivity (35~W/m$\cdot$K axially, 1~W/m$\cdot$K radially) to reflect the strongly directional heat conduction of cylindrical cells. Two housing variants were compared: solid-walled and ventilated (with slots on the front face). Side-face ventilation holes were found to have no meaningful effect on cell temperatures, as the stagnation zone is primarily on the front surface facing the direction of motion. Front ventilation holes reduced cell temperatures from 35~\textdegree{}C to 26~\textdegree{}C. Housing material conductivity (PETG vs.\ expensive high-conductivity filament) had no significant effect on cell temperature, indicating conduction through the housing is not the limiting thermal resistance.

These results were cross-validated against third-party P45B characterisation data showing cell surface temperatures of approximately 40~\textdegree{}C under 30--50~A pulsed discharge --- our pack operates at lower duty cycle and peak current, making the simulated 26~\textdegree{}C plausible. \section{Conclusions and Recommendations}

\subsection{Conclusions}

Exoni demonstrates that a custom-designed powered knee exoskeleton meeting the conflicting constraints of high torque density, low backdrive resistance, passive thermal safety, and minimal mass is achievable within a capstone project scope. The simulation-driven design methodology --- progressing from electromagnetic FEA to motor thermal CHT to battery thermal CHT --- was essential: none of the three key design decisions (stator frame, rotor configuration, battery configuration) could have been made correctly by intuition alone.

The final system meets all five design constraints. Most notably, the Halbach rotor configuration reduced backdrive torque by $6\times$ relative to an iron-backed default while simultaneously reducing rotor weight by 2.88$\times$, and the battery thermal analysis enabled a configuration change from 6s2p to 8s1p, saving approximately 300~g. The second motor iteration achieved torque constant within 5\% of simulation, validating the electromagnetic model after accounting for 2D simulation limitations in the first iteration.

The three custom PCBAs were successfully designed and validated, resulting in safe operation with the custom battery pack. The custom firmware successfully brought up all subsystems: ADC current-offset calibration, AS5048A encoder reads at 1~kHz, dual IMU reads at 200Hz, and closed-loop FOC commutation of the custom BLDC motor. In closed-loop mode, $i_d$ converged to near zero and $i_q$ tracked its reference with low steady-state error, confirming correct encoder-to-electrical-frame alignment and well-tuned PI current controllers. The binary telemetry stream provided a continuous 200Hz data feed for validation and future system integration.

\subsection{Recommendations for Future Work}

Several directions are identified for future development of the Exoni platform.

On the mechanical side, further mass reduction through carbon fibre structural components and a higher gear ratio would improve torque density while reducing overall system weight below 1.5~kg.

On the firmware and control side, the most impactful near-term improvement would be the implementation of sensorless FOC using back-EMF observers, eliminating the encoder cable and connector as a potential failure point in outdoor use. Additionally, the current PI current controller gains were tuned empirically; a model-based auto-tuning procedure using the identified motor electrical parameters ($R$, $L$) would make commissioning faster and more repeatable for future hardware iterations. Extending the firmware to support bilateral operation (two motor drives communicating over a low-latency bus) and adding a real-time safety monitor for the impedance output bounds would be necessary prerequisites for clinical validation trials. The gait classifier was trained on single-subject MOCAP data; expanding the training set to multiple subjects and additional terrain types (stairs, ramps, uneven ground) would improve generalisation. Longer term, an online adaptive impedance update via iterative learning control or reinforcement learning could personalise assistance without manual re-tuning.

The training dataset for the gait classifier consisted of trials from a single subject collected in a single MOCAP session.
Classifier generalisation is fundamentally limited by training set diversity: a model that has only seen one subject's kinematics will overfit to their specific stride length, cadence, and joint angle ranges.
Collecting additional MOCAP sessions across multiple subjects, body weights, and stair geometries would directly improve accuracy and lack of samples is likely the reason for current poor accuracy of the model (87 \%).
Automated labelling via the existing gait event markers (LFO, LTO, RFO, RTO) from MOCAP data makes adding new sessions low-effort once data is collected. The current pipeline will 
automatically clean and label this mocap data for cross-reference with its corresponding Exoni on-device data.

The current gait classifier is scoped to a single activity type of stair ascent. 
A natural extension would be a hierarchical two-stage classification architecture: a top-level activity classifier first identifies the type of locomotion (stair ascent, stair descent, level walking, ramp incline, ramp decline), and a second-stage phase classifier, specific to the identified activity, then segments that activity into its gait phases. 
This mirrors how biomechanics actually differ across activities. The appropriate impedance parameters for swing phase during stair descent are fundamentally different from those during level walking. 
Each activity would have its own phase-parameter lookup table, with the top-level classifier gating which table is active. 
This architecture keeps per-activity models small and interpretable while scaling to arbitrary numbers of activity types without redesigning the control pipeline.

\subsection{Gait Feature Vector}

\noindent\textbf{Raw features (indices 0--12):} The 13 raw features are a direct snapshot of the sensor readings at the current 200\,Hz tick: the knee encoder angle and the three-axis accelerometer and gyroscope readings from both IMUs (IMU1 on the primary board near the motor, IMU2 on the separate IMU board at the lower leg).

\noindent\textbf{Windowed features (indices 19--44):} The remaining 26 features are the per-channel windowed mean and standard deviation computed over a 10-sample (50\,ms) sliding window for 13 channels: \texttt{enc\_deg}, \texttt{imu1\_ax/ay/az}, \texttt{imu1\_gx/gy/gz}, \texttt{imu2\_ax/ay/az}, and \texttt{imu2\_gx/gy/gz}. These capture short-term temporal structure that the instantaneous raw readings cannot, allowing the classifier to distinguish phases that have similar instantaneous sensor values but different trends. Scale factors were auto-generated by \texttt{label\_stair\_data.py} from the training dataset's per-feature maximum absolute value.

\subsection{Impedance Control Torque-to-Current Derivation}

The calculation from measured knee state to FOC current reference is derived below.

\paragraph{Step 1 — Spring-damper torque.}
\begin{equation}
    \tau = K(\theta_\text{des} - \theta) + B(\dot{\theta}_\text{des} - \dot{\theta}) \quad [\text{Nm}]
\end{equation}
where $K$ (Nm/deg) and $B$ (Nm$\cdot$s/deg) are the phase-dependent stiffness and damping constants selected from Table~\ref{tab:phase_params}, $\theta$ is the measured knee angle from the encoder (deg), and $\dot{\theta}$ is the finite-difference angular velocity (deg/s). The output $\tau$ is clamped to $\pm18$~Nm before proceeding.

\paragraph{Step 2 — Backdrive friction compensation.}

A fixed term overcomes the measured gearbox resistance:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_\text{bd} = \begin{cases} +0.26~\text{Nm} & \dot{\theta} > +5~\text{deg/s} \\ -0.26~\text{Nm} & \dot{\theta} < -5~\text{deg/s} \\ 0 & |\dot{\theta}| \leq 5~\text{deg/s} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The deadband prevents sign-flip chatter when the joint is stationary. The 0.26Nm value is the measured peak cogging torque of the assembled motor and gearbox.

\paragraph{Step 3 — Torque to q-axis current reference.}
\begin{equation}
    I_q = \frac{\tau + \tau_\text{bd}}{K_t \cdot N_\text{gear}} \quad [\text{A}]
\end{equation}
where $K_t = 0.97$Nm/A is the measured torque constant from the second motor iteration (within 5\% of simulation) and $N_\text{gear} = 8.4$ is the planetary gearbox ratio. The result is clamped to $\pm10$~A before being written to the 1~kHz FOC inner loop.
